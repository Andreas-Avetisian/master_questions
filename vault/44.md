# Provide the definition of Trustworthy AI as given by the HLEG. Explain the concept of Controllable AI.

## Trustworthy AI according to the HLEG (slide 67)

The Kieseberg lecture does not use a single one-line sentence to define trustworthy AI; it defines it operationally, through the **seven key requirements** published by the EU **High-Level Expert Group on AI (HLEG)**. An AI system is considered "trustworthy" if, throughout its lifecycle, it satisfies all seven:

1. **Human agency and oversight**
2. **Technical robustness and safety**
3. **Privacy and data governance**
4. **Transparency**
5. **Diversity, non-discrimination and fairness**
6. **Environmental and societal well-being**
7. **Accountability**

The lecture deliberately places this HLEG list next to the NIST characteristics (*valid and reliable; safe; secure and resilient; accountable and transparent; explainable and interpretable; privacy-enhanced; fair with harmful bias managed*) to show that the European and US frameworks converge on the same handful of properties, with slightly different emphasis (HLEG stresses societal/environmental well-being and diversity; NIST stresses explainability and bias management).

In short: for the HLEG, **trustworthy AI = AI that simultaneously fulfils these seven requirements**, and the slide immediately following (*Some criticism*) makes clear that the list is the working definition the lecture adopts.

## Controllable AI (slides 69-73)

The lecture introduces **Controllable AI** as the engineering response to the trustworthiness requirements. Its core idea fits on a single slide:

- **Detect when an AI system deviates too much**.
- **Re-calibrate the system or shut it down**.

In other words, a controllable AI is one that can be *monitored* for deviation from expected behaviour and *intervened upon* (adjusted or switched off) when it drifts. The lecture then unpacks this into three building blocks.

**Detection (slide 71)**, mechanisms for noticing that something has gone wrong:

- *Sanity checks* on inputs and outputs.
- A *corrective model* trained on alternative data to flag discrepancies.
- *Explainability*, so deviations are interpretable rather than opaque.
- *Critical data surveys* to verify that training and production data still match assumptions.

**Reaction (slide 72)**, mechanisms for acting once a deviation is detected:

- *"Divine rules"*, hard-coded constraints the model cannot override.
- *Training of clearly defined non-targets*, teaching the model what it must *not* do.
- *Destructive backdoors / kill switch*, to shut the system down cleanly.
- *Intentional bias / logical backdoor*, a pre-baked correction path.
- *Fail-safes and logical bombs*, to limit damage when other safeguards fail.

**Support (slide 73)**, transparency measures that make control possible at all:

- *Transparency in the use of AI* towards users.
- *AI-less alternative* as a fallback when the AI cannot be trusted in a particular case.
- *Transparent data sources*.
- *Transparent data management*.

So Controllable AI is the practical counterpart to Trustworthy AI: the first says *what* an AI system must be, the second says *how* you keep it that way in operation, by combining detection, reaction and supporting transparency.

## Short summary

The **HLEG** defines **trustworthy AI** through a list of seven key requirements that an AI system must meet throughout its lifecycle: *human agency and oversight, technical robustness and safety, privacy and data governance, transparency, diversity/non-discrimination and fairness, environmental and societal well-being, and accountability*. **Controllable AI** is the operational discipline that keeps an AI system within those requirements: it detects when the system deviates too much from expected behaviour and then re-calibrates it or shuts it down, backed by *detection* mechanisms (sanity checks, corrective model, explainability, critical data surveys), *reaction* mechanisms (divine rules, non-targets, kill switches, fail-safes), and *support* measures (transparency in use, AI-less alternatives, transparent data sources and management).
