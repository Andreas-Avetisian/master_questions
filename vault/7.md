# Ex CEO Ballmer laughs at the early iPhone saying "500 Dollars -that is the most expensive phone in the world. And it doesn't appeal to business customers, because it doesn't have a keyboard." What does that statement have to do with radical innovations vs. incremental innovations and customer needs?

Ballmer judged the first iPhone with the logic of **incremental innovation** and with the expectations of **existing business customers**.

- **Incremental innovation** improves known products for existing markets. Here, customer behavior and articulated needs are easier to assess.
- **Radical innovation** is rare, risky, has no close predecessor, and often addresses **not-yet-articulated customer needs** or even new customers/markets.
- Because of that, **market research is limited**: it mainly captures what customers already know and can already express. It is much weaker at detecting what people will value in a fundamentally new solution.

That is exactly why Ballmer misjudged the iPhone:

- He focused on **existing needs** of current business-phone users: lower price, keyboard, established business use.
- He overlooked that a radical innovation can create value through **new forms of use** that customers had not clearly asked for yet.
- So he evaluated a potentially radical product with **incremental criteria**.

**Core point for the exam:**

Ballmer's statement shows that radical innovations are often underestimated by incumbents because they do not fit current customer expectations. Existing customers usually articulate what they already know, while radical innovations often succeed by addressing **non-articulated needs** and changing the market itself.
