# Describe the steps of the Innovation Process

## Starting point: what an innovation process is

The lecture defines the innovation process as a **structured, systematic, multi-phase approach** for turning new ideas into value. Key properties from the slides:

- Structure can be managed, measured, monitored and improved.
- No universal process exists; every company needs to adapt one.
- It has to allow for **process loops, process exits and room for the unexpected**.
- It is the job of **Innovation Management** to set up and support this process, define risk / opportunity handling, set responsibilities and monitor success.

## Layer 1: Simplified Innovation Process (5 steps)

**1. Idea Generation**

- Generate a *large* number of new ideas, not every idea will make it.
- Internal sources: suggestion systems, R&D, customer support, hotline, complaints.
- External sources: competition, other markets, tech developments, trends, market research institutes, customers, crowd sourcing.

**2. Idea Selection**

- Feasibility check against criteria: technical feasibility, economic viability, resource availability.
- Evaluation tools: cost-benefit analysis, decision tree analysis.

**3. Idea Realisation**

- Turn the idea into a *concept, design, prototype*.
- Deep dive into the idea and think about future steps.
- This phase becomes the template for the actual product / process realisation.

**4. Test**

- "Final rehearsal": test concept, product, name, packaging, UX.
- Collect (potential) customer feedback.
- Decide if modifications are needed. Testing is expensive, so it has to be planned.

**5. Market Entry**

- High cost for production and marketing; good communication of information is crucial.
- Typical pitfalls: wrong assessment of customer behaviour, opposition from retail, competition blocking, and disturbance of operations (capacity, logistics, bottlenecks).

## Layer 2: Innovation Process Scheme (detailed view)

The simplified 5 steps are a subset of a richer scheme:

1. **Innovation Trigger** : a problem, a gap between actual and target state; identified via situational analysis, market observation and spotting opportunities.
2. **Obtaining of Ideas** : collection of existing ideas (customers, suppliers, employees, competition), promotion of new solutions, catalogued in an "idea database" and screened for fit.
3. **Evaluation** : pursue promising ideas, drop the rest, *interdisciplinary* assessment, rank the most promising.
4. **Selection (Decision)** : decide what will be realised; often decided by top management, separate from the evaluation group.
5. **Realisation** : either as a project (resource-heavy, high novelty) or integrated into a routine process (lower complexity).
6. **Market Entry** : the point at which the **invention becomes an innovation**; deploy the 4Ps of marketing, adjusted to company strategy, ahead of entry.

**Innovation Controlling** is drawn as a **cross-sectional process** in the slide: it plans, steers, coordinates and controls innovation activities across *all* phases, aggregating information for decision-making.

## "Reality" caveat from the slides

The lecture explicitly warns that in practice:

- A clear separation of phases is often not possible.
- Some steps are skipped, some are repeated.
- Parallel activities create interdependencies: **Simultaneous Engineering** pushes such parallelisation on purpose, removing department lines, cutting waiting times and defining clear interfaces between steps.

## Short summary

According to `VO_02_Innovation.pdf`, the innovation process has a **simplified 5-step view**: Idea Generation → Idea Selection → Idea Realisation → Test → Market Entry. A more detailed **Innovation Process Scheme** expands this into: Innovation Trigger → Obtaining of Ideas → Evaluation → Selection → Realisation → Market Entry, with **Innovation Controlling running cross-sectionally** over all phases. In reality, phases may be skipped, repeated, or run in parallel (Simultaneous Engineering), and the whole process is coordinated by Innovation Management.
