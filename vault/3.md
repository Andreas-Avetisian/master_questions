# Name the general quality criteria for science from the MRI lecture and describe 5 of them in detail together with eventual problems discussed in the lecture.

## General quality criteria from the lecture

- Honesty
- Objectivity
- Verifiability
- Reliability
- Validity
- Comprehensibility
- Relevance
- Logical argumentation
- Originality
- Reproducibility

Peer review is presented as an important safeguard, especially for verifiability, but not really as a separate quality criterion.

## Five criteria in detail

1. **Honesty:** Scientific work must present facts truthfully and must not misrepresent sources, methods, or results. The lecture explicitly mentions plagiarism, deliberate false statements, data manipulation, and falsification or invention of results.

   Problems discussed: classic misconduct includes copying without attribution, changing data to fit a thesis, and inventing findings. These actions destroy trust in science and can have serious consequences for the author and for later research built on false results.

2. **Objectivity:** Research should be as free as possible from prejudice, personal interests, and emotional bias. The scientist should argue neutrally and not simply side with a cause because it seems morally right.

   Problems discussed: the lecture warns about hidden basic attitudes, emotional writing, implicit premises that are never justified, conclusions driven by the author's own interests, prejudicial judgement without evidence, and one-sided literature selection. Related examples of "scientific lies" include cherry-picking data points, participants, or methods.

3. **Verifiability:** Scientific claims must be checkable by others. Results become more trustworthy when experts review them and when other researchers can examine and challenge them over time.

   Problems discussed: verification fails when papers do not provide enough detail. The lecture stresses that all relevant details, logical steps, data, and materials or software must be available. A specific problem mentioned is poor data availability in many IT-security publications; sometimes data is only visible to reviewers, which limits later checking.

4. **Reliability:** A method, instrument, or procedure is reliable if it produces stable and reproducible results. Reliability is therefore about consistency, not just about getting an appealing result once.

   Problems discussed: reliability is threatened by fuzzy measured values, unstable algorithms, poor calibration, and poor maintenance of instruments or systems. Even if a result looks plausible, it is weak if the measurement process is not stable.

5. **Validity:** Validity asks whether the study actually measures what it claims to measure. A method can be reliable but still invalid if it consistently measures the wrong thing.

   Problems discussed: the lecture highlights confusion between correlation and causality, overly broad answer ranges in surveys, imprecise questions, non-representative samples, tendency of respondents toward mean values, and vaguely defined terms. All of these can make a study answer a different question than the intended one.

## Short exam summary

The lecture names honesty, objectivity, verifiability, reliability, validity, comprehensibility, relevance, logical argumentation, originality, and reproducibility as general quality criteria for science. Five central criteria are honesty, objectivity, verifiability, reliability, and validity; their typical failure modes are misconduct and plagiarism, bias and one-sided argumentation, lack of transparency and missing data, unstable or poorly calibrated methods, and measuring the wrong construct or confusing correlation with causality.
