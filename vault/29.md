# What is the classical triad of algorithm ethics? Can you briefly talk about each of its parts and what they each entail? What other aspects of algorithm ethics are there which are not covered by the triad?

The classical triad of algorithm ethics is **transparency, accuracy, and accountability**.

Useful memory aid:
- **Transparency** = can we understand what the system does?
- **Accuracy** = does it work correctly and reliably enough?
- **Accountability** = who is responsible, and how can compliance be shown?

## The Triad

### 1. Transparency
Transparency means that affected persons, professionals, and regulators should receive essential information about the AI system and its data processing.

It includes:
- information about the system's purpose, operation, and consequences
- traceability and, where possible, explainability
- communication about performance limits, testing, and relevant system properties

Why it matters:
- people can only exercise their rights, challenge decisions, or use the system appropriately if they understand enough about it
- it helps reduce the "black box" problem, even if full explanation is not always possible

### 2. Accuracy
Accuracy means that the AI system should produce sufficiently correct, reliable outputs for its intended purpose.

It includes:
- accurate and up-to-date data where relevant
- appropriate performance metrics
- robustness against errors, faults, and problematic situations
- validation and testing of the system

Why it matters:
- inaccurate systems can produce unfair, incorrect, or discriminatory outcomes
- accuracy is therefore not just a technical issue, but also an ethical one

### 3. Accountability
Accountability means that a responsible actor must be identifiable and able to demonstrate compliance with legal and ethical requirements.

It includes:
- documentation and record-keeping
- monitoring, auditing, and traceability
- quality-management processes
- reporting of incidents or malfunctions
- clear allocation of responsibilities and mechanisms for correction or redress

Why it matters:
- if harm occurs, it must be clear who answers for it and how problems are detected and fixed

## What The Triad Does Not Fully Cover

The triad is important, but it does **not** cover the whole field of algorithm ethics. The slides point to broader frameworks for trustworthy AI. Additional important aspects include:

- **fairness and non-discrimination**: avoiding unfair bias, unequal treatment, and stigmatization
- **explainability / explicability**: related to transparency, but more specifically about making decisions understandable
- **human autonomy and oversight**: humans should remain able to make meaningful choices and supervise AI systems
- **prevention of harm / safety**: systems should not cause physical, mental, social, or institutional harm
- **privacy and data governance**: respect for privacy, data quality, data integrity, and access rules
- **technical robustness and security**: resilience against attacks, faults, and misuse
- **societal and environmental well-being**: effects on democracy, society, sustainability, and the environment
- **stakeholder participation and redress**: involving affected groups and providing remedies when things go wrong

## Short Exam Version

The classical triad of algorithm ethics consists of **transparency, accuracy, and accountability**. Transparency requires that the functioning and implications of an AI system are communicated in an understandable way. Accuracy requires that the system works correctly and reliably enough for its intended purpose and does not generate avoidable errors. Accountability requires that responsible actors can be identified and can prove compliance through documentation, monitoring, and oversight. The triad does not fully cover other major ethical concerns such as fairness and non-discrimination, explainability, human autonomy, privacy and data governance, safety and robustness, and societal or environmental impacts.
