# Provide 6 typical roles (except visitors/author) in an academic conference and describe their tasks.

The "Typical Roles" slide (page 7) enumerates roles such as Conference Organizer, Conference Chair, Local Organizer, Workshop (Co-)Chairs, Program Committee, Proceedings Chair, Steering Committee, Webmaster, Social Event Chair, Keynote Chair and more. Six of these are picked out below, focusing on the ones the lecture actually describes in detail.

## 1. Conference Organizer

Sits at the top of the organisational pyramid, together with an **organization team**, and *"has the financial responsibility"* for the conference. This role is the one signing contracts, running the budget, and making sure the event as a whole (venue, proceedings, social programme) comes together. Without this role, a conference has no legal or financial anchor.

## 2. Conference Chair (General Chair and PC-Chair)

The Conference Chair is the scientific face of the conference. Under this umbrella the lecture lists the **General Chair** and the **PC-Chair(s)**. The review-process slides implicitly describe the PC-Chair's tasks: entering key topics into the review system, running the **bidding process**, handling conflicts of interest, **assigning papers** (typically minimum three reviewers per paper), setting review deadlines, chasing reviewers, running the discussion phase, sending out accept/reject decisions, managing shepherding and the timeline until camera-ready. The General Chair complements this by steering the overall scientific programme and representing the conference externally.

## 3. Local Organizer

Described in detail on page 13, *"especially important in case of 'wandering' conferences."* Their tasks are explicitly listed:

- *"Organizes the venue"*.
- *"Takes care of insurance, transportation, ..."*.
- Social events, together with a Social Event Coordinator when available.
- *"Can even generate sponsoring and such"*.

They work with a **local team**, often juniors and students, who handle the front-line work like help desk and registration. The lecture stresses that the Local Organizer has *"no financial responsibilities"* (those sit with the Conference Organizer), but is judged on whether the venue works, break rooms and areas are adequate, and the social programme "brands" the installment.

## 4. Program Committee (PC)

The research-quality backbone of the conference, covered on pages 8 to 11. The slide "Program Committee" states: *"Almost in every conference. Main reviewers. Not including sub-reviewers, not including editorial reviewers."* The reward is symbolic: *"No money, no points, just a bit of fame."*

Responsibilities are explicitly named as **reviewing and discussion**. In practice this means: bidding on papers ("Want to", "neutral", "Cannot", "Conflict of interest"), writing the actual reviews (typically three per paper), commenting across reviewers, then participating in the **discussion phase** led by a **lead reviewer**, staying polite during disagreement, and finally agreeing on clear acceptances or rejections, sometimes with shepherding for conditionally accepted papers.

## 5. Workshop (Co-)Chair(s)

Covered on page 12. The lecture distinguishes two setups:

- **Workshops as part of the main conference**: separate but integrated deadlines, a subset or different PC, must follow the general rules of the conference, typically appear in the same proceedings (clearly distinguishable, often fewer pages).
- **Fully separate co-located events**: different rules and deadlines, potentially a different publisher, different committees, but sharing the venue, local organizers and social events with the main conference.

The Workshop (Co-)Chair is responsible for the call for papers, review process, and programme of the workshop, while coordinating with the main-conference chairs on anything that has to align (dates, proceedings, space).

## 6. Social Event Chair

Listed on page 7 and motivated by the separate "Social Events" slide (page 16). Their task is to design and run the conference's social programme (welcome reception, conference dinner, excursions). The lecture flags this as a *"major thing that you should attend"* and, importantly, one that *"makes your installment memorable"*. Practical responsibilities include managing the social-event budget, handling additional tickets for accompanying colleagues or partners, and coordinating with the Local Organizer on logistics.

## Short summary

Six typical conference roles described in the lecture are: the **Conference Organizer** (financial and overall responsibility, heads the organization team); the **Conference Chair / PC-Chair** (scientific leadership, runs the review pipeline from topic setup through bidding, paper assignment, discussion and final decisions); the **Local Organizer** (venue, insurance, transportation, sponsoring, local team of juniors and students, no financial responsibility); the **Program Committee** (main reviewers, responsible for reviewing and discussion, rewarded only by reputation); the **Workshop (Co-)Chair** (organises a workshop embedded in or co-located with the main conference, aligning rules, deadlines and proceedings); and the **Social Event Chair** (designs and runs the social programme that "brands" the conference). Together they split the financial, scientific, logistical and cultural workload of running an academic conference.
