# What is known as the "AI Winter" and what was the reason for it?

> Note: the term **"AI Winter" is not used in the lecture slides**. The AI intro deck only shows a history timeline without discussing downturns. The answer below is standard AI history as background knowledge; the one concrete slide hook is Minsky & Papert's 1969 *Perceptrons* critique in the Multilayer Perceptron deck, which is the accepted cause of the first winter.

## What the AI Winter is

**AI Winter** is the term used for **periods of reduced funding, interest and progress in AI research**, following waves of hype in which the field had over-promised and under-delivered. After each hype cycle, investors, governments and the public lost confidence, money dried up, and AI research slowed for years. There are conventionally two main AI winters.

## First AI Winter (roughly 1974 – 1980)

Causes:

- **Perceptron controversy**: Minsky and Papert's 1969 book *Perceptrons* showed that a single-layer perceptron cannot solve non-linearly separable problems (e.g. XOR). This effectively killed funding for neural networks for years.
- **Lighthill Report (1973, UK)**: a harshly critical assessment that claimed AI had failed its ambitions, which triggered large cuts to UK AI funding.
- **DARPA cuts in the US**: after the Speech Understanding Research programme failed to meet its targets.
- **ALPAC report (1966)**: judged machine translation a failure, cutting MT funding.
- Hardware and data of the time were simply too weak for the claims researchers had made.

## Second AI Winter (late 1980s – early 1990s)

Causes:

- **Collapse of the LISP machine market (1987)**: specialised AI hardware was suddenly beaten by cheaper general-purpose workstations.
- **Expert systems failed to scale**: they were brittle, required enormous manual knowledge engineering, and could not handle cases outside their rule base. The "knowledge acquisition bottleneck" was never solved.
- **Japan's Fifth Generation Computer project** did not deliver its promised breakthroughs.
- **DARPA Strategic Computing Initiative** pulled funding when results did not meet expectations.
- Commercial disillusionment: companies had bought into AI products that did not pay off.

## Why it keeps happening

The same underlying pattern recurs:

- **Over-hyped promises** set unrealistic expectations.
- **Limited compute, data or theoretical foundations** at the time cannot back those promises.
- **Funding bodies and the market** reverse sharply when the promises miss.
- A quieter phase of research follows, which later produces the next breakthrough (backpropagation in the 1980s, deep learning in the 2010s).

## Short summary

AI Winter = a phase of **collapsed funding and interest in AI**, caused by the field over-promising and then failing to deliver. The **first winter (mid-1970s to around 1980)** followed the perceptron critique, the Lighthill report and the ALPAC / speech-recognition failures. The **second winter (late 1980s to early 1990s)** followed the collapse of the LISP-machine market and the failure of expert systems to scale. The root cause is always the same: hype exceeds what current hardware, data and methods can actually deliver.
