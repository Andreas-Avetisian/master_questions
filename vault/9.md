# You are asked to design a systematic literature review on a topic you are familiar with. Explain how the literature review should be constructed so that it does more than summarize prior work and instead identifies a defensible research gap and justifies the proposed study.

A strong systematic literature review should be built as a transparent search-and-synthesis process, not as an author-by-author summary.

1. Start with a clear review objective and research question(s) before searching.
2. Predefine the databases, search terms, time limits, and inclusion/exclusion criteria.
3. Document screening and selection with a PRISMA-style process, including duplicates removed and reasons for exclusion.
4. Extract comparable information from each study, such as methods, sample, context, and findings.
5. Synthesize across studies thematically or conceptually, comparing agreements, contradictions, limitations, and missing contexts.
6. Derive the research gap from the pattern of evidence, for example a missing population, setting, method, or unresolved contradiction.
7. End by showing exactly how the proposed study closes that gap and why that contribution is needed.

The key point is that the gap must come from systematic evidence, not from a vague claim that “no one has studied this yet.”
