# Visualisation, eg during ideation and prototyping is critical for Design Thinking. What are specific advantages of visualising your ideas as early as possible (name at least five)?

## Why early visualisation matters

The Module 5 deck opens with the John Maeda quote *"If a picture is worth 1.000 words, a prototype is worth 1.000 meetings."* That sets the frame: a visual artefact, even a very rough one, carries more information per minute than any description. Visualising early turns an idea into something the team and users can **see, react to and argue with**.

## Specific advantages (directly from the decks)

**1. Replaces lengthy discussion, creates a shared understanding.**

The "picture worth 1.000 words, prototype worth 1.000 meetings" principle. A drawing or prototype gives the team a single reference point, so discussions converge faster instead of people defending different mental images.

**2. Gets feedback as fast and as early as possible.**

The process explicitly says: *"Fail early and kill your darlings → Get feedback on your ideas and prototypes as fast and as early as possible."* Early visuals invite criticism at a stage when changes are still cheap.

**3. Enables exploration, communication and collaboration with stakeholders.**

The first purpose of a prototype in the deck is *"Explore: communicate, collaborate → Discuss solutions with team and stakeholders."* Without a visual, stakeholders have nothing concrete to react to, and feedback stays vague.

**4. Manages risk by checking assumptions early.**

The iteration slide is explicit: *"When you iterate, you manage risk by repeatedly checking your assumptions and answering more refined questions."* A visual exposes which assumptions are shaky, before they become expensive mistakes.

**5. Saves time and cost in the long run.**

*"Iteration actually saves you time in the long run"* (Module 5 iteration slide) and *"Save costs and gain even more insight about your customer"* (testing slide). Cheap visuals early prevent expensive rebuilds later.

**6. Checks value and functionality in an early stage without large resources.**

Said literally on the Wizard of Oz slide: *"Easy possibility to check value and functionality in an early stage without needing too much resources."* You can test whether the idea even creates value before you build the real thing.

**7. Supports decision-making, presentation and persuasion.**

The second purpose of a prototype is *"Evaluate: present, persuade, inspire → Decide on the best possible option."* Visuals let the team rank options concretely and convince others, which cannot be done with pure text.

**8. Enables learning through trial and error.**

*"Learn through trial and error… sharing and receiving feedback… will help you improve your idea."* Each visual is a cheap experiment, and the experiments are what move the idea forward.

## Short summary

Early visualisation matters because **"a prototype is worth 1.000 meetings"**: it builds shared understanding, invites fast feedback, lets you **collaborate with stakeholders, manage risk by checking assumptions, save time and cost, test value with minimal resources, support decisions and presentations,** and above all **learn by trial and error**. In Design Thinking, an idea that is not visualised cannot really be challenged, tested or improved.
