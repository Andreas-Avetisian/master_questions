# Explain the following terms for research methods: Inductive, deductive, quantitative, qualitative, primary, secondary. Give three examples for combinations of at least two of these methods. Explain the issues with inductive and deductive methods.

The six terms form three pairs.

**Inductive vs. deductive** (direction of reasoning)

- **Inductive (bottom-up):** from many individual observations, a pattern is spotted, a hypothesis formulated, and a theory developed. Repeated observation raises the probability that the theory is true.
- **Deductive (top-down):** start from a theory; derive hypotheses and predictions; confirm or refute them through many individual observations. A hypothesis that survives many refutation attempts becomes the currently accepted theory.

**Quantitative vs. qualitative** (type of data)

- **Quantitative:** numerical data, focus on measurements (statistics, experiments, structured surveys).
- **Qualitative:** non-numerical, focus on meaning and depth (interviews, observations, focus groups).

**Primary vs. secondary** (who collected the data)

- **Primary:** data that do not yet exist are collected directly by the researcher for the specific investigation.
- **Secondary:** existing data / surveys from other researchers are compiled, compared, reviewed and evaluated (e.g. studies, meta-studies).

## Three example combinations

1. **Qualitative + primary:** conducting new in-depth interviews with users of a banking app.
2. **Quantitative + secondary:** a meta-analysis of published customer-satisfaction surveys.
3. **Deductive + quantitative:** hypothesising "feature X improves retention" and testing it with an A/B experiment.

## Issues

**Induction:**
- Are the observations truly independent?
- Is the entire relevant spectrum covered, or only a biased slice?
- Does bias enter the measurement?
- Risk of confusing correlation with causality.

**Deduction:**
- Are the experiments meaningful for the hypothesis?
- Is the researcher biasing the experiment through their own behaviour?
- Are we really measuring what we claim to measure?
