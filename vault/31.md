# What are the main issues when it comes to algorithmic decision support and accountability/responsibility?

Algorithmic decision support creates accountability problems because decisions may be partly automated, but responsibility still has to remain identifiable, explainable, and challengeable.

## Main issues

- **Unclear allocation of responsibility:** It may be unclear whether responsibility lies with the developer/provider, the deploying organisation, or the human user. A key requirement is that the accountable entity for an AI-supported decision must be identifiable.
- **Opacity / "black box" problem:** Many systems are hard to explain. If the logic of a decision cannot be made understandable, it becomes difficult to justify the result and to check whether it respected rights and legal principles.
- **Need for auditability and traceability:** Accountability requires mechanisms to monitor AI-based decisions and outcomes, document how they were reached, and demonstrate compliance in audits or reviews.
- **Bias, discrimination, and errors:** AI systems can produce unfair or incorrect outcomes. Accountability therefore includes ongoing evaluation to detect and rectify biases, errors, and unintended consequences.
- **Need for human oversight:** Decision support should not replace meaningful human control. Humans must remain able to supervise, question, and, if necessary, override the system.
- **Reporting harm and redress:** Trustworthy AI requires minimising and reporting negative impacts, considering trade-offs, and providing redress when people are adversely affected.
- **Governance and documentation duties:** Accountability is not just moral but organisational: quality management, testing, record-keeping, incident reporting, and clearly assigned internal responsibilities are needed.

## Short exam summary

The core problem is that AI-supported decisions can be opaque, biased, and hard to contest, while responsibility is often spread across several actors. Therefore, trustworthy decision support needs clear responsibility, transparency/traceability, human oversight, monitoring, and effective redress.
