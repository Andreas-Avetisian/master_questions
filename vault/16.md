# Describe the different scale levels of data and give an example for each of them.

## The scale levels

Scale level tells you **which operations are allowed** on a variable. There are four main levels (plus absolute as a subtype of ratio), grouped into two families:

**Categorical scales** (no meaningful distances):

**1. Nominal** : values are just *names*, no order, no arithmetic.

- Allowed: =, ≠, counting.
- *Example*: car colour (red, blue, green), zip code, gender, customer ID. Zip codes look like numbers but they are names, subtraction makes no sense.

**2. Ordinal** : values have a *natural order*, but differences between them are not meaningful.

- Allowed: =, ≠, <, >, sort, median.
- *Example*: military ranks (private < sergeant < captain), Likert scale (strongly disagree → strongly agree), customer satisfaction grades (A, B, C).

**Metric / cardinal scales** (meaningful distances):

**3. Interval** : order *and* meaningful differences, but **no natural zero**, so ratios do not make sense.

- Allowed: above plus + and − and arithmetic mean.
- *Example*: temperature in °C (20°C is not "twice as warm" as 10°C), clock time, calendar years. Zero point is arbitrary.

**4. Ratio** : order, meaningful differences *and* meaningful ratios, because there is a natural zero that means "absence of the attribute".

- Allowed: all arithmetic, including multiplication, division, geometric mean.
- *Example*: weight (0 kg = no weight, 80 kg is twice 40 kg), height, age, income, distance.

**(5. Absolute)** : a subtype of ratio where the unit itself is fixed by nature, typically counts.

- *Example*: number of employees, number of website visits, pieces in a box.

## Why scale level matters

- It determines which **statistics** are valid: mode for all, median from ordinal upwards, arithmetic mean only from interval upwards, geometric mean only for ratio.
- It determines which **analyses** are valid: a linear regression between one nominal and one ordinal variable (see the "how to fail" slide in MRI) is a classic questionnaire mistake.
- It determines which **encoding** is needed for ML: label / one-hot for nominal, ordinal encoding for ordered categories, direct use for metric.

## Short summary

Four main levels: **Nominal** (names, e.g. car colour), **Ordinal** (ordered but no distances, e.g. military ranks), **Interval** (ordered, meaningful distances, no natural zero, e.g. °C), **Ratio** (ordered, distances and ratios, natural zero, e.g. weight). Absolute is a fixed-unit subtype of ratio, typically counts. Nominal and ordinal are *categorical*, interval and ratio are *metric*. The level fixes which operations, statistics and ML encodings are legitimate.
