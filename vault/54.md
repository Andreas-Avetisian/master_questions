# When describing facts or theories in your text, what is important for your readers?

## The core principle: the reader must never be in doubt about the status of a claim

The style lecture devotes an entire slide ("Facts vs Theory", page 9) to exactly this question. The key sentence:

> *"Origin of facts or results: A reader of your text should never wonder (or believe wrongly or be confused) whether ..."*

and then four cases are distinguished. This means every time an author states something in the text, the reader should be able to tell immediately *what kind of statement it is* and *where it comes from*. The slide lists four distinct cases:

### 1. An undisputable, already known fact

Example from the slide: *"Function xyz of chip abc when called with parameters (10,11) crashes."* If this is a known fact, **provide a reference to the initial discovery [REF]**. The reader should be able to verify the fact in the original source.

### 2. An idea, proposal, theory or quote that belongs to someone else

**Provide a reference [REF] to where and by whom it was introduced previously.** Never present foreign ideas as if they were yours; this is essential both for reader orientation and to avoid plagiarism.

### 3. A fact you just discovered yourself

Here no prior reference exists, so the burden shifts to you: **describe in the text the discovery itself, what led you to it, and give instructions that let others reproduce it.** Reproducibility is what makes it a fact instead of a claim.

### 4. A new idea or theory you are proposing yourself

**Show the evidence and reasoning you base it on, and the incremental steps that produced the insight.** A theory without evidence is an opinion; the reader must be able to follow your chain of reasoning.

## Supporting rules on the surrounding slides

The "Facts vs Theory" slide does not sit in isolation. The same deck adds three reinforcing points:

- **Level of knowledge (page 8, "Absolutisms")**: do not claim to know more than you do. The slide contrasts *"BAD: 'Everyone thinks software xxx is insecure'"* with *"GOOD: 'To the best of our knowledge the approach presented here'"* and *"GOOD: 'We believe this to be the first combination of x and y to achieve ...'"*. Hedging honestly is part of being trustworthy.
- **Speculation (page 10)**: refrain from random speculation inside the main content. *"Either it works or it did not work (for you), and you already know this when your paper / thesis is finished."* Speculation belongs in the Outlook / Future Work section, not in the description of facts and theories.
- **Citation quality (pages 20-22)**: the reader must be able to trust the references. Prefer international, peer-reviewed conferences or journals; do not cite Wikipedia, student theses, random blog posts, or company whitepapers; electronic sources only if the author is an established researcher. The lecture also reminds: *"just because something was accepted, presented and published at an international conference does not make it a truth, it is one puzzle piece of a larger ongoing discussion."*
- **Plagiarism (page 23)**: *"Never claim the work and thoughts of others as your own. Provide proper references to earlier works and their results."*

## Why this matters for the reader (papers-theses deck, pages 13 and 16)

The companion deck frames the same expectation from the reader's side: an academic reader reads a text *"to get smarter, learn from it"* and the research process relies on *"scholarly conversation"* where *"researchers respond to each other's work, challenge assumptions, refine understanding, citations / referencing."* A reader can only participate in that conversation if the text cleanly separates established fact, prior theory, and the author's own new claim; otherwise they cannot verify, challenge, or build on the work.

## Short summary

When describing facts or theories, the reader should never have to guess what kind of statement they are looking at. The lecture's "Facts vs Theory" slide formalises this into four cases: (1) an established fact needs a reference to its original discovery, (2) someone else's idea, theory or quote needs a reference to the person and place of introduction, (3) a new fact you discovered needs a description of how you found it and a path for others to reproduce it, (4) a new theory you propose needs the evidence and incremental reasoning behind it. Around this core, the text should **avoid absolutisms** (use "to the best of our knowledge" instead of "everyone thinks"), **keep speculation out of the main content**, rely on **high-quality, peer-reviewed citations** (no Wikipedia, no company whitepapers), and **never plagiarise**. In short: *transparency about the status and origin of every claim*, so the reader can verify, trust, and build on what they read.
