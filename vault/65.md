# When developing research projects, how do you define measurements and risks?

When developing a research project, **measurements** and **risks** should both be derived from the actual project structure. They should not be generic add-ons.

## Measurements

Measurements should be defined from the **targets** and expected **results** of the project.

- Start from the main target and sub-targets / research questions.
- Ask what observable evidence would show that these targets were reached.
- Use measurements that fit the actual project knowledge and the data that will realistically be available.
- Keep them understandable and convincing for reviewers.
- In practice, they can be tied to an **impact-chain** or to concrete project elements such as workpackages, deliverables, milestones, or results.

So a short exam answer is: measurements should be **goal-oriented, data-based, and reviewer-acceptable**.

## Risks

Risks are a normal part of a proposal and should be defined in a **project-specific** way.

- Do not list generic risks that would fit any project.
- Define risks in relation to the concrete work, methods, partners, or expected results.
- Place them either:
  - directly at the **workpackage level**, or
  - in an overarching **novelty / risk** section.
- Each risk should be linked to suitable **countermeasures / mitigation**.

The proposal should stay concise: the slides explicitly treat risks as a proposal element, but also warn against including an overly **fine-tuned risk analysis**.

## Exam Formula

Measurements show whether the project targets are being reached, so they should be derived from the goals, expected results, and available evidence. Risks should be project-specific and can be placed either per workpackage or in a dedicated risk section, always together with countermeasures.
