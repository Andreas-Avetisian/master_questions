# The survey paper assignment requires students to present the state of the art, identify trends, discuss contradictions or gaps, and document the literature-search methodology in PRISMA-like form. Design an answer strategy for such a paper and explain how you would judge whether the paper reflects mature research thinking rather than mere compilation.

## Answer strategy for the survey paper

A survey paper is a **Systematic Literature Review (SLR)**, so the work splits into method and synthesis.

**1. Scope and research question.** Define a clear, focused, answerable research question; fix inclusion / exclusion criteria *before* searching (publication date, language, peer-reviewed, topical fit).

**2. Search methodology (the PRISMA-like part).**

- Databases: IEEE Xplore, ACM DL, Scopus, SpringerLink, Google Scholar.
- Build search strings in blocks of synonyms, linked with AND / OR.
- Log, for each database, the query, the date, and the number of hits.
- Merge, deduplicate (Zotero / EndNote), then apply inclusion / exclusion.
- Report the funnel in a **PRISMA flow diagram** (identified, screened, included, excluded with reason).

**3. State of the art.** Group the included papers by theme or sub-problem, not by author. For each cluster, describe what is established, by whom, and on what evidence.

**4. Trends.** Look across time: which methods are rising, which are fading, where the field is moving (e.g. shift from rule-based to transformer models; emerging benchmarks, new datasets, regulation).

**5. Contradictions and gaps.** Apply a meta-level to the cluster: where do papers disagree, where are results not reproducible, which questions are untouched. These gaps are what justify new research, the "space" you carve out for your own work.

**6. Synthesis and conclusion.** Do not just list findings; tie them back to the research question and show the implication for practice or for further research.

## How to judge "mature research thinking" vs mere compilation

A compilation reads like a bibliography with sentences between entries. A mature paper shows judgement. Concrete markers:

- **The paper argues, it does not just summarise.** There is a thread from question → method → findings → conclusion.
- **Papers are compared**, not listed. The author points out *why* two studies disagree, not just that they do.
- **The method is transparent and reproducible**: PRISMA diagram is present, search terms and dates are given, inclusion / exclusion criteria are justified.
- **Critical stance**: weaknesses of individual studies are named (sample size, methodology, missing replication), not hidden.
- **Gaps are specific and actionable**, not "more research is needed".
- **Own voice**: the author's interpretation is distinguishable from the sources, typically visible in framing sentences, topic clusters, and the conclusion.
- **Citations are purposeful**, every cited paper earns its place, no citation padding.
- **Limitations of the review itself** are acknowledged (languages excluded, databases not covered, publication bias).

## Short summary

Write the survey in two layers: a **documented, PRISMA-style method** (question, databases, search strings, inclusion / exclusion, flow diagram) and a **synthesising narrative** (themed state of the art, trends, contradictions, gaps, conclusion). The paper is mature when it *argues and compares* instead of listing, when the method is reproducible, when gaps are specific, when the author's voice is visible, and when the review's own limitations are acknowledged. If any of these are missing, it is a compilation, not a review.
