# Identify and discuss the key ethical principles for trustworthy artificial intelligence as proposed by the European Union. Evaluate their significance in the context of modern AI systems.

The EU High-Level Expert Group (HLEG) identifies seven key requirements for trustworthy AI:

1. Human agency and oversight: AI should support human autonomy rather than replace human judgment completely. People must be able to supervise, intervene, or override systems when necessary.
2. Technical robustness and safety: AI should be reliable, secure, accurate, resilient to attacks, and safe in operation, with fallback plans if something goes wrong.
3. Privacy and data governance: Personal data must be protected, and data should be collected, managed, and accessed responsibly. Data quality and integrity are part of this requirement.
4. Transparency: AI systems should be traceable and explainable enough that people understand when AI is being used and on what basis decisions are made.
5. Diversity, non-discrimination, and fairness: AI should avoid unfair bias and work fairly across different groups, with attention to accessibility and inclusive design.
6. Environmental and societal well-being: AI should benefit society, democracy, and the environment rather than create broader social or ecological harm.
7. Accountability: There must be clear responsibility for AI outcomes, including auditability, monitoring, reporting of harms, and possibilities for redress.

Significance for modern AI systems:

- These principles remain highly important because current AI systems are deployed at scale in sensitive areas such as hiring, finance, healthcare, education, security, and public administration.
- Human oversight is essential because modern systems can automate or strongly influence high-stakes decisions, but still make errors, reflect poor assumptions, or be misused.
- Robustness and safety are especially important for systems that can hallucinate, fail unpredictably, or be attacked through adversarial inputs, data poisoning, or prompt manipulation.
- Privacy and data governance matter because many AI systems depend on large datasets, often containing personal or sensitive information.
- Transparency and accountability are critical because many advanced models are difficult to interpret; without documentation and traceability, it is hard to challenge decisions or assign responsibility.
- Fairness is central because biased training data can reproduce or amplify discrimination against underrepresented groups.
- Environmental and societal well-being has become more significant as large-scale AI consumes substantial computing resources and can affect public discourse, democratic processes, and labor markets.

Evaluation: the EU principles are significant because they turn AI ethics into practical design and governance requirements. They provide a strong framework for assessing whether AI is not only effective, but also socially acceptable and legally defensible. Their main limitation is that they are high-level and can conflict in practice, for example transparency versus privacy or accuracy versus fairness. Therefore, modern AI systems need these principles to be operationalized through testing, documentation, oversight, and regulation rather than treated as abstract ideals.
