# Critically explain the role of PRISMA in systematic literature reviews. What problems does it solve, what does it not solve, and how could a researcher still conduct a rigorous review if they had never heard of PRISMA?

## What PRISMA is

**PRISMA** = **P**referred **R**eporting **I**tems for **S**ystematic Reviews and **M**eta-**A**nalyses. It is an evidence-based minimum set of items for reporting systematic reviews, originally aimed at reviews evaluating the effects of interventions.

Four parts:

1. **Checklist** : the items that must appear in the paper.
2. **Flow Diagram** : documents records identified, included, excluded, with reasons.
3. **Statement** : the short guideline document itself.
4. **Explanation and Elaboration** : detailed rationale and examples for each item.

## What problems PRISMA solves

- **Reporting quality and transparency** : forces the author to state search strings, databases, inclusion / exclusion criteria, and exactly how many papers dropped out at each stage.
- **Reproducibility** : another researcher can redo the same search and arrive at a comparable set of papers.
- **Bias reduction** : by demanding that exclusions are justified, it makes cherry-picking visible.
- **Structure** : gives a concrete skeleton for the review paper, so writers are not inventing the structure from scratch.
- **Comparability** : different PRISMA-compliant reviews can be compared, because they report the same items.

## What PRISMA does *not* solve

- It does not judge **whether the search strategy itself is good** : a poor query in the wrong databases still passes PRISMA if it is documented.
- It does not replace **domain expertise** : PRISMA cannot tell you whether an excluded paper was actually relevant, or whether the included ones are the important ones in the field.
- It does not do the **content analysis or synthesis** : interpreting what the included literature actually *says* is still the researcher's job.
- It does not guarantee **quality of the primary studies** reviewed (that is the role of separate risk-of-bias tools).
- It is a reporting guideline, not a research method : following the checklist does not by itself make the underlying review rigorous.

## Doing a rigorous review without PRISMA

Even without knowing the acronym, a researcher can hit the same quality bar by applying the underlying principles:

- **Write a protocol first** (research question, scope, inclusion / exclusion criteria) and, ideally, pre-register it.
- **Document the search** : databases used, exact queries, date of search, filters applied.
- **Log every paper** and the reason it was kept or dropped, so the funnel is reconstructible.
- **Use multiple reviewers** or at least a second pair of eyes for screening, to control individual bias.
- **Assess study quality** explicitly (e.g. risk-of-bias criteria), not just count papers.
- **Report limitations** of the search and of the synthesis honestly.
- **Peer-review the method** before running it, so flaws show up before the data is locked in.

## Short summary

PRISMA is a **reporting guideline**, not a recipe for good research. It solves the *transparency, reproducibility and bias-documentation* problem by forcing a standard checklist and flow diagram, but it does not fix a weak search, replace expert judgement, or do the actual synthesis. A researcher who never heard of PRISMA can still produce a rigorous review by applying the same principles: upfront protocol, fully documented search and exclusions, multiple reviewers, explicit quality assessment, and honest reporting of limitations.
