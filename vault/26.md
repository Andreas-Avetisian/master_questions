# What is the difference between a high and a low fidelity prototype? Describe two specific prototyping methods that represent each of these types.

## Fidelity in a prototype

In the Module 5 deck, **fidelity** is treated as a deliberate choice, "Choose the fidelity" is one of the steps when planning a prototype (p. 13). Fidelity refers to **how close the prototype is to the finished product** in terms of detail, look, feel and interactivity.

- **Low-fidelity** prototype: rough, cheap, quick to build, not interactive (or only minimally). Goal is to *explore, communicate and collaborate*, to check the basic idea or value proposition.
- **High-fidelity** prototype: polished, looks and behaves close to the real product, often clickable / usable. Goal is to *evaluate, present, persuade*, to test usability, desirability and viability more rigorously.

Trade-off from the deck: low-fi is fast and iteration-friendly but limited in what it can test; high-fi gives richer feedback but takes time and is "useless if your prototype does not create any value for your customers" (p. 11-12).

## Two low-fidelity methods

**1. Paper Mockups (p. 23-24)**

- Hand-drawn screens, flows or interfaces on paper.
- *"Can be done quickly by anyone; iterations easily possible."*
- Tools mentioned: Marvel app to bring paper screens to life.
- Use: sketching UI layouts and user flows before any code is written.

**2. Wireframing (p. 25)**

- Explicitly described in the slide as a *"Simple, low-fidelity prototype for digital products."*
- Usually non-interactive: shows structure, layout and key buttons, not visual styling.
- Use: agreeing on information architecture and screen layout across the team.

(Also in this family on the earlier slides: Mood Board p. 17, Storyboard p. 18.)

## Two high-fidelity methods

**1. Click Dummy (p. 26)**

- Explicitly described as *"Higher fidelity and often clickable version of an interface."*
- Users can navigate between screens, so realistic flow and interaction can be tested.
- Typically built in tools like Figma (referenced on the same page).

**2. Mock Sale & Infiltrator Technique (p. 71)**

- Explicitly described as *"Create a high fidelity prototype and place it in a store shelf. See how people are interacting with the prototype."*
- The product looks real enough that shoppers treat it as a real product on a shelf.
- Tests desirability in a realistic purchase environment.

(Wizard of Oz, p. 22, is another high-fi-feeling method: the user thinks they are using a working product while a hidden operator drives it.)

## Short summary

**Low-fidelity** = rough, cheap, fast, mainly for *exploring and iterating*, e.g. **Paper Mockups** (p. 23-24) and **Wireframing** (p. 25, explicitly "low-fidelity prototype for digital products"). **High-fidelity** = polished, realistic, mainly for *evaluating and persuading*, e.g. **Click Dummy** (p. 26, explicitly "higher fidelity and often clickable") and **Mock Sale & Infiltrator Technique** (p. 71, explicitly "high fidelity prototype"). The Module 5 deck frames fidelity as a conscious choice, matched to what you still need to learn: lower fidelity early to iterate, higher fidelity later to test realistic behaviour.
