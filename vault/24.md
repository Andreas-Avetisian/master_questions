# Describe all steps of the Design Thinking Process. Discuss why the Process is to be conducted as a repeating cycle rather than a linear programme.

## What Design Thinking is (slide definition)

> "Design Thinking is a **mindset, culture and process** to create products, services and business models based on **extensive human-centred research and iterative prototyping**." (Leifer, Stanford; quoted in both decks)

The core goal is to develop a solution that is **desirable, viable and feasible** for the user, starting from the customer and their problem.

## The 5 steps of the Design Thinking Process

From `VO_03_Design-Thinking-Recap.pdf` (p. 8-9) and `241104_Design Thinking_Module1.pdf` (p. 27-29):

**1. Empathize** : *Develop a deep understanding of the challenge.*

- Walk in the customer's shoes, observe, interview, gather qualitative data.
- Tools used in the course: interviews, Fly-on-the-Wall, Dispatch Center, beliefs worksheet.

**2. Define** : *Clearly articulate the problem you want to solve.*

- Synthesize the research into a sharp problem statement.
- Tools: personas, "How might we…" questions, knowns / unknowns.

**3. Ideate** : *Brainstorm potential solutions; select and develop your solution.*

- Generate as many ideas as possible (create choices), then converge on the most promising (make choices).
- Quantity first, no early judgement, build on others' ideas.

**4. Prototype** : *Design a prototype (or a series of prototypes) to test all or part of your solutions.*

- Cheap, fast, low-fidelity artefacts to make the idea tangible.
- The prototype is a tool for learning, not the final product.

**5. Test** : *"Engage in a continuous short-cycle innovation process to continually improve your design."*

- Put the prototype in front of real users, observe, collect feedback.
- Feed results back into earlier steps.

Alongside this, both decks frame the process inside the **Double Diamond** (VO_03 p. 7, Module 1 p. 22-29): *Discover → Define → Develop → Deliver*, i.e. diverge to understand, converge on the problem, diverge on solutions, converge on the one to ship.

## Why the process is a repeating cycle, not a linear programme

The slides are explicit on this. Four reasons, all taken directly from the decks:

- **The definition itself is iterative.** Design Thinking = "extensive human-centred research and **iterative prototyping**" (VO_03 p. 3, Module 1 p. 19). Iteration is built into the definition.
- **The Test step is defined as a loop.** Test is not an end-point but "a **continuous short-cycle** innovation process to continually improve your design" (Module 1 p. 29, VO_03 p. 8). Results flow back into Empathize / Define / Ideate / Prototype.
- **Course rule 6**: "Work in an iterative and interdisciplinary way: more steps and more perspectives make you stronger" (Module 1 p. 35-36). It is listed as one of the standing rules of the method.
- **Fail fast, learn fast / Fail early and kill your darlings** (VO_03 p. 3-4 guidelines, Module 1 p. 34). Early failure is only valuable if the process lets you *go back* and adjust: that is exactly what a cycle allows and a linear programme does not.

Practical consequences of being cyclic:

- Test results can send you back to *Define* (the problem was wrong), to *Ideate* (the idea doesn't work) or to *Prototype* (the artefact is the issue).
- It fits wicked / ambiguous problems where the right solution cannot be known up front.
- It enforces humility: you assume your first framing is incomplete.

## Short summary

The Design Thinking process has **five steps**: **Empathize** (understand users), **Define** (sharpen the problem), **Ideate** (generate solution options), **Prototype** (make ideas tangible cheaply), **Test** (run the prototype with users and learn). It is framed inside the **Double Diamond** (Discover, Define, Develop, Deliver). The process is run as a **repeating cycle**, not linear, because the method itself is defined around iterative prototyping and "continuous short-cycle" testing, because one of the explicit rules is "work in an iterative and interdisciplinary way", and because the "fail fast, kill your darlings" mindset only pays off if you can loop back to earlier steps after each test.
