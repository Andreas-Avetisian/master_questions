# Explain Immanuel Kant's concept of the categorical imperative. Discuss its central formulations and demonstrate its application using a concrete example.

## The idea behind the categorical imperative

Kant's ethics is a **deontological** (duty-based) theory, essentially based on his *Foundations of the Metaphysics of Morals* (1785). The lecture stresses two features:

1. **Consequences of an action are morally irrelevant.** Kant: *"an action performed from duty does not have its moral worth in the purpose which is to be achieved through it but in the maxim by which it is determined."* What matters is the *maxim* (the underlying rule of action), not the outcome.
2. **Moral duty means following the moral law, which must be rational and universal.** This universal moral law is exactly what Kant calls the **categorical imperative**: it applies unconditionally, to every rational being, regardless of personal inclination.

The slides label the categorical imperative as Kant's *"ultimate ethical principle"*, an *"acid test"* of right and wrong and a *"moral compass"*.

## The two formulations taught in the course

**First formulation, the "Generalization formula"**:

> *"Act only according to that maxim whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law."*

The moral quality of an action is judged by the criterion whether it is possible to elevate it to a maxim and generalize it. In short: *what if everybody did what you are doing?* If the universalised maxim becomes self-contradictory or self-defeating, the action is morally forbidden.

**Second formulation, the "Self-purpose formula"**:

> *"Act in such a way that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, never merely as a means to an end, but always at the same time as an end."*

This prohibits instrumentalizing a human being, reducing the person to a mere thing, because it threatens their freedom and dignity. Persons must always also be recognised as ends in themselves.

## Concrete example: the false promise (directly from the slides)

*"Person A makes a promise but intends to break it if it serves his or her purposes."*

Applying the **generalization formula**, the maxim becomes: *"If it serves my purposes, I will make a promise with the intention of breaking it if advantageous."*

Universalized, this maxim is self-contradictory: *"If everyone would break promises, the whole idea of promising would collapse; there would be no such thing as a 'promise' because in such a climate, anyone making a promise would lack credibility."* The institution of promising presupposes that promises are generally kept, so a maxim of deceitful promising destroys the very practice it tries to exploit. The action is therefore morally forbidden.

Applying the **self-purpose formula** confirms this: the deceitful promiser uses the other person merely as a means to his or her own ends, without their informed consent, which violates their dignity as a rational agent.

From this single "acid test", Kant derives further concrete duties: to keep contracts, to tell the truth, to avoid injury to others.

## A note on the limits (acknowledged in the lecture)

The slides also point out a well-known **problem with Kant's philosophy: its rigidity**, for example in cases where two moral laws collide (classic lie-to-the-murderer dilemma). The categorical imperative gives a strong, universal test, but it does not always resolve conflicts between duties.

## Short summary

The **categorical imperative** is Kant's ultimate, unconditional moral law, grounded in reason rather than in consequences. It has two central formulations in the course slides: the **generalization formula** ("act only on a maxim you can will to become a universal law") and the **self-purpose formula** ("treat humanity never merely as a means, but always also as an end"). A lying promise fails both tests: universalising it destroys the practice of promising, and it uses the other person purely as a means. Kant's test therefore condemns the action as morally forbidden, though its rigidity can make conflicts between duties hard to resolve.
