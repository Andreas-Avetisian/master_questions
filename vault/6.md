# Name and explain eight of the twelve flaws one should avoid when creating a questionnaire.

The lecture lists 12 general flaws. All 12 below; pick any 8 to explain in the exam.

1. **Questions too broad / unspecific** — e.g. "What do you think of cereal?" gives no usable data because it allows any kind of answer.
2. **Answer options not MECE** (not mutually exclusive, not covering all cases) — e.g. "How often do you eat cereal? [multiple times a day / daily / every few weeks / rarely / never]" leaves no option for "a few times a week".
3. **Unclear order / meaning of answers** — e.g. "Rarely, often, sometimes, always" — respondents can't tell what is between "rarely" and "sometimes".
4. **No thought about coding and analysis** — mixing scale levels (e.g. one question ordinal, another nominal) makes the intended analysis (e.g. linear regression) impossible afterwards.
5. **Two or more questions combined into one** — e.g. "Which cereal do you prefer and how often do you buy it?" produces answers that can't be separated.
6. **Too many questions** — respondents lose attention; after a few introductory questions, focus on the relevant aspects only.
7. **Personal questions at the beginning** — e.g. starting with "How much do you earn a year?" causes drop-offs and distrust; personal items belong at the end.
8. **Not explaining unusual topics** — jargon like "knowledge graph", "reinforcement learning", "firewall" may be obvious to you but not to respondents, leading to noise or non-responses.
9. **Bias in the questions** — leading or loaded wording pushes respondents toward an answer, e.g. "Climate change has become a very important topic and not caring about the environment will destroy the earth. What is your opinion on fossil fuels?"
10. **Questions / answers not based on (literature) research** — e.g. age categories invented ad hoc instead of using established scales; always reuse existing validated scales where they exist.
11. **No pre-tests** — the questionnaire has not been tested on a pre-test sample from the general population, so problems with wording, order, or flow only appear after real data collection.
12. **Questions asked "just because"** — every question must serve the research topic; padding with nice-to-know items costs attention and dilutes the data.

## Bonus: related (non-questionnaire-specific) biases from the same slides

- **Interviewer bias** — the interviewer influences the answers (tone, personal interest, demographics). Counter with written/online interviews for critical topics, neutrality reminders, multiple interviewers, training.
- **Analysis bias** — different analysts would clean and process the data differently. Counter by specifying the analysis method before data collection and letting multiple people analyse.
- **Interpretation bias** — the same result is interpreted differently depending on personal beliefs. Counter by defining in advance what "good" vs. "bad" results mean and where the dividing lines sit.
