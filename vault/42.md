# Compare and contrast utilitarian ethics and deontological ethics. In your answer, outline the fundamental principles of each theory and refer to key representatives.

## The underlying split: teleological vs deontological

The lecture anchors the comparison in a more general distinction:

- **Teleological** (from Greek *telos* = goal, end): rightness or wrongness of an action *depends on its consequences* and whether they achieve the purpose in question (e.g. happiness).
- **Deontological** (from Greek *deon* = duty or basic moral rights): rightness or wrongness *does not depend on the consequences*; deontological theories consider actions to be *intrinsically* right or wrong and emphasize principles of duty and obligation (e.g. don't lie).

Utilitarianism is the paradigmatic teleological theory; Kant's moral duty framework is the paradigmatic deontological one.

## Utilitarian ethics

Utilitarianism, also called **consequentialism**, takes the *optimization of consequences* as its fundamental principle. Its core tenets from the slides:

- The right course of action is the one that *promotes the general good*.
- The general good is described in terms of **utility**: the principle of utility is the foundation of morality and the ultimate criterion of right and wrong.
- Utility = *net benefits* created by an action; the moral end is *"the greatest possible balance of good and evil."*
- Moral principle (Spinello): *"Persons ought to act in a way that promotes the maximum net expectable utility, that is, the greatest net benefits or the lowest net costs, for the broadest community affected by their actions."*
- Bentham's own definition of ethics: *"the art of directing men's actions to the production of the greatest possible quantity of happiness, on the part of those whose interest is in view."*

On a practical level, utilitarianism calls for moral decisions through *rational, objective cost/benefit analysis*.

**Key representatives**: Jeremy Bentham (1748-1832) and John Stuart Mill (1806-1873).

**Problem flagged by the lecture**: for utilitarianism there are *no intrinsically unjust or immoral acts*. The theory lacks sensitivity to vital ideals of justice and human rights, since any act can in principle be justified by a sufficiently large aggregate benefit.

## Deontological ethics

The moral-duty framework focuses on *obligation and duty*. It is a rule-based approach, essentially based on Kant (1724-1804) and his *Foundations of the Metaphysics of Morals*. Its core tenets from the slides:

- Consequences are morally irrelevant: *"an action performed from duty does not have its moral worth in the purpose which is to be achieved through it but in the maxim by which it is determined."*
- Moral duty means following the *moral law*; that law must be *rational and universal*.
- The universal moral law is expressed in the **categorical imperative**, Kant's ultimate ethical principle.
- First formulation (generalization formula): *"Act only according to that maxim whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law."*
- Second formulation (self-purpose formula): *"Act in such a way that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in the person of any other, never merely as a means to an end, but always at the same time as an end."* This protects human freedom and dignity against instrumentalization.

From the categorical imperative Kant derives concrete duties: to keep contracts, to tell the truth, to avoid injury to others.

**Key representative**: Immanuel Kant. The slides also mention Spinello's cyberethics textbook as the teaching reference.

**Problem flagged by the lecture**: *rigidity*. In cases where two duties or two laws conflict, Kant's framework struggles to give guidance.

## Direct comparison (via the trolley problem, page 70)

The lecture operationalises the contrast through the footbridge variant of the trolley dilemma:

- **Utilitarian action**: push the man off the footbridge to save five lives.
- **Deontological alternative**: do not push.
- **Utilitarian rule**: *"always choose the action that maximizes the number of lives saved."*
- **Deontological rule**: *"never act if the act results in harming people who were not threatened if you had not acted."*

This is the clearest shorthand for the two theories. Utilitarianism counts lives and aggregates welfare; deontology refuses to instrumentalize the individual even for a greater numerical good.

## Short summary

**Utilitarian ethics** is a *teleological/consequentialist* theory: an act is right if it maximizes net utility or "the greatest happiness of the greatest number", developed by **Bentham and Mill**. **Deontological ethics** is duty-based: an act is right if it conforms to a rational, universal moral law regardless of consequences; its canonical form is **Kant's categorical imperative**, with the generalization formula and the self-purpose formula as its two central formulations. The trolley problem captures the clash: utilitarianism pushes the man to save five; deontology refuses to use him as a means. The lecture also flags each theory's weakness: utilitarianism's indifference to intrinsic rights, and Kant's rigidity in conflicts of duty.
